Player Development

How players at this age think, feel, and learn — the foundation of effective coaching.

Cognitive Development

Piaget's Preoperational Stage — logical and rule-based thinking has not yet developed
Children this age are genuinely egocentric — they cannot yet take another person's perspective
Attention span: 2–5 minutes maximum per activity — this is neurology, not a behavior problem
Imaginative and magical thinking is dominant — use it in every activity
The "bee swarm" around the ball is completely normal and developmentally appropriate
Right-left differentiation is still developing — do not rely on it for instruction

Social and Emotional

Parallel play is the norm — children this age play alongside each other, not truly together
Approval from parents and the coach is the primary motivator
Fear of failure exists even at 4–6 — create safety to try, fail, and try again freely
Winning and losing have very little meaning — the experience itself is everything
Friendships are beginning but remain highly fluid week to week

What This Means for Coaching

Every player must have their own ball — waiting in lines is harmful at this age
Keep instructions to three words or fewer: "Big kick!" or "Fast feet!"
Use imagination and theme-based language throughout every activity
Never separate a child from the group as discipline — use a quiet conversation in a visible location with another adult nearby
Praise the effort and the attempt — never only the outcome
If a child is disengaged, sit quietly beside them in a visible area — never force participation

Play-Practice-Play Model▼
Play-Practice-Play

The approach used throughout CYA recreational programs. Learning in context transfers more reliably to games than isolated drilling.

1
PLAY
Start with a game. Let them play freely. Observe what needs attention. Do not coach yet.
→
2
PRACTICE
Pause the game. Address one specific skill through a focused activity. Brief and directly relevant.
→
3
PLAY
Return to the game. Players apply what they just practiced in a real context.

Traditional

Warm up → Drill → Drill → Drill → Game

Skills in isolation often fail to transfer. Players do not understand the context.

Play-Practice-Play

Game → Identify → Practice → Game

Learning happens in context. Transfer to match situations is measurably higher.

Progressive Model (Unopposed → Opposed)▼
The Progressive Model (Unopposed → Opposed)

Used within the Practice phase of Play-Practice-Play. Every skill should be introduced simply and built toward game-realistic pressure in three steps.

1
UNOPPOSED
Technical Foundation. Player performs the skill without a defender. Focus purely on correct technique. No pressure, no decision-making yet.
Example: Dribble through cones · Pass against a wall · Shoot at empty goal
→
2
SEMI-OPPOSED
Decision-Making Introduced. A passive or shadow defender is added. The player reads a situation and makes a choice. Technique may break down — that is expected.
Example: 1v1 with a jog-only defender · Dribble with shadow who cannot tackle
→
3
FULLY OPPOSED
Game-Realistic Pressure. Live, competitive defender. Full pressure. Mistakes happen — use them as teaching moments. This is where learning is cemented.
Example: 1v1 live · Small-sided game · Match play

Common Mistake

Skipping Steps 1 and 2 because "it is just a rec session."

Even 5 minutes unopposed sets the tone, builds confidence, and gives your coaching points something to attach to.

Key Principle

Players need to feel success at each step before pressure is added.

Jumping to fully opposed work before technique is established creates frustration and sloppy habits. Build the foundation, then test it.

Sample 45-Minute Session▼
Sample 45-Minute Session (U4-U6)

Combining Play-Practice-Play with the Progressive Model in a single session.

TimePhase / Model StepWhat It Looks Like
0-7 min
PLAY (PPP Phase 1)
Free dribbling game — observe who can turn, who holds the ball too long, who avoids pressure.
7-12 min
PRACTICE — Step 1 (Unopposed)
Cones dribbling activity. One coaching point: "use the inside of your foot to turn."
12-20 min
PRACTICE — Step 2 (Semi-Opposed)
Same activity with a shadow defender. Players must now decide when and where to turn.
20-30 min
PRACTICE — Step 3 (Fully Opposed)
1v1 or 2v2. Live pressure. Coach from outside — use freezes sparingly.
30-45 min
PLAY (PPP Phase 3)
Small-sided scrimmage. Let them apply it. Encourage, do not instruct.

No more than 3 players waiting in a line at any time
All fitness through playing soccer — no laps, no static stretching
Game Day Format▼
Game Day Format — U4-U6

Game days are split into two parts: a team practice block and a continuous play block. 2v2 across 4 small simultaneous fields — every player is in action at the same time.

TimeBlockWhat Happens
0-25 min
Team Practice
Fun, structured games and activities run by the coach. Dribbling games, tag games, small competitions. High energy, lots of touches.
25-55 min
Continuous Play
Players split onto 4 small fields and play 2v2 games. When a ball goes out, coaches immediately roll a new ball in — play never stops. No throw-ins, kick-ins, or goalkeeper restarts.
55-60 min
Wrap-Up
Bring everyone together. High-fives, one fun recap, send them off with energy.

⚖ Working with Officials
The Foundational Rule
Your players are watching how you respond to every call. You are teaching them how to handle adversity, authority, and disappointment — not just soccer.
Before the Game
Introduce yourself and learn the official's name. This creates a positive dynamic for the entire game.
During the Game
Never argue a call. You may ask one polite question: "Can you help me understand that call?" Once, calmly, at a stoppage.
After a Difficult Call
Turn to your players: "Keep playing — we focus on what we control." How you respond teaches more than any call.
After the Game
A genuine thank-you to the official is good modeling for your players and more appreciated than you might expect.
Serious Procedural Errors
Address formally through your coordinator after the game. Never escalate on the field.
Player Reactions
Acknowledge the feeling, then redirect. "I hear you. Twenty minutes left — let's use them."
Parent Sideline Issues
Move to them immediately and quietly: "This stops now." Then document it and report to your coordinator.
Context
At recreational level, most officials are volunteers — often teenagers. They will make mistakes. Model grace. Your players are watching.
⚠ Conflict Resolution
The 3-Second Pause
Before every response to conflict, pause for 3 seconds. The pause is where good coaching happens. Reactive responses almost always make things worse.
Player vs. Player Conflict▼
Allow minor disputes to play out — resolving small conflicts is an important life skill
Intervene immediately for physical contact, name-calling, or exclusionary behavior
Get down to their level — kneel when speaking to young players
Use feelings language, not blame: "it feels unfair when..." rather than "you did..."
Hear both perspectives briefly before speaking
Redirect to the activity quickly — dwelling extends the conflict
For U13+: short conversation in a visible, open area with another adult nearby
Script: "Let's pause for a second. I want to hear both of you. [Player A], what happened from your side? ... [Player B], what did you see? ... OK. Here is what we are going to do next."
Player Refuses to Participate▼
Never force participation — it consistently backfires
Give a calm choice: "You can stand with me for two minutes, then we can try together"
Check in quietly — sometimes there is something larger going on
Conduct private conversations in a visible, open area with other adults nearby
Contact the parent privately after practice — not in front of the child
For U4–U6: a parent joining on the field briefly is appropriate
Allow the player to rejoin when ready, without drawing any attention
Script: "I can see something is off today — that is completely fine. You can stand here with me until you are ready. No rush at all."
Sideline Parent Issues▼
Never address sideline behavior during a game — wait until after
Approach the parent away from other adults and all players
Begin with recognition: "I appreciate how much you care about [child's name]"
Describe the specific behavior — not a character judgment
Explain the impact on the child's focus and the team
Establish a clear mutual expectation going forward
If it continues, contact your program coordinator
Script: "Hey [name], do you have a moment? I know you are passionate — that genuinely helps. When coaching from the sideline happens during the game, [child] receives conflicting instruction. Could we try cheering everyone during the game instead? I think it would really benefit [child]."
Discipline and Player Behavior▼
Natural consequences are always more effective than punitive consequences
Brief time-out (30–60 seconds) for young players — not extended benching
Never use physical exercise as punishment — this creates a lasting negative association with fitness
For U9+: short calm conversation in an open, observable area with another adult present
Document any serious incidents and share with your program coordinator
Consistent standards matter more than the severity of consequences
The goal is learning, growth, and continued engagement with the sport
Script: "I need to talk with you for a moment. What happened back there? ... I hear you. Here is what needs to be different: [specific behavior]. When we come back next week, we start fresh. Deal?"
Your Own Frustration as a Coach▼
You will have difficult days — that is human and nothing to be ashamed of
If emotions are rising, pause 10 seconds before responding — this is professionalism
Your emotional state affects every player on that field, whether you speak or not
No session outcome is worth damaging a player's relationship with the sport
After a difficult session, talk to a fellow coach or your coordinator — do not carry it alone
Self-compassion is required for sustained good coaching
Script: Remind yourself: "My primary job is to make sure they want to come back next week. Everything else is secondary."

SafeSport Standards

These standards protect the players you work with and protect you.

Required Reading
US Soccer and CYA require all coaches to be familiar with SafeSport standards. These standards exist because the vast majority of harm to young athletes occurs in private, one-on-one situations. The standards below are designed to eliminate those opportunities entirely.
Two-Deep Leadership
Never be alone with a minor — ever. A minimum of two adults must be present at all practices, games, and events.
Open and Observable
All activities and conversations with players must occur in open, observable, interruptible settings. Closed vehicles and private spaces are never appropriate.
Electronic Communication
Do not send private one-to-one messages to minor players. All digital communication involving players should include a parent or guardian.
Appropriate Physical Contact
High-fives, fist bumps, and group celebrations are appropriate. Avoid extended physical contact. When in doubt, do not.
Transportation
Never transport a minor alone in a vehicle without another adult present.
Locker Rooms
Adults must never be alone with a minor in a locker room, bathroom, or changing area.
Reporting Obligations
If you observe, suspect, or are told about any form of abuse or boundary violation involving a minor, report it immediately. Contact your coordinator and relevant authorities. Do not investigate — report.
Your Own Protection
Following these standards protects the players you work with and protects you. A coach who follows them never has to worry about their own conduct being questioned.
If You Are Unsure
Ask yourself whether you would be comfortable if the player's parent could see exactly what you are doing and why. If yes: proceed. If no or maybe: do not do it. If you observe something concerning, report it.

Player Development

How players at this age think, feel, and learn — the foundation of effective coaching.

Cognitive Development

Transitioning into Concrete Operational Thinking
Beginning to understand rules and fairness — "that's not fair!" is developmentally appropriate
Can follow two-step instructions reliably for the first time
Attention span: 5–10 minutes per focused activity segment
Cause-and-effect thinking is emerging: "if I do this, that happens"
Competition awareness is building but remains mostly intrinsic

Social and Emotional

"Best friends" become very important — honor and work with these connections
Beginning to compare themselves to peers — handle corrections thoughtfully
The concept of a team is starting to form but remains fragile
Fairness is a strong emotional trigger — be consistent and transparent
Rule-following feels important and children will police each other
Some children begin showing leadership instincts for the first time

What This Means for Coaching

Keep segments to 5–8 minutes maximum before transitioning
Lots of variety maintains engagement — predictability leads to disengagement
Introduce rules with simple, honest explanations
Celebrate individual improvement explicitly: "you were not doing that last week — look at you now!"
Light competitive elements with emphasis on personal improvement, not defeating others
Begin building team culture intentionally: team names, routines, and shared rituals matter

Game Day Format▼
Game Day Format — U7-U8

Structured small-sided games with basic rules. Teams split across multiple simultaneous fields so everyone plays the full session.

U7 — 3v3
Team splits across 3 fields. All 3 play simultaneously. No goalkeeper. 2 halves with a short break. Throw-ins and kick-ins apply.
U8 — 4v4
Team splits across 2 fields. Both play simultaneously. No goalkeeper. 2 halves with a short break. Throw-ins and kick-ins apply.

Your Role on Game Day

Player Check-In: Take attendance as players arrive. Note any injuries. Make sure every child is accounted for.
Sideline Conduct: Stay positive and encouraging. No tactical shouting during play. Your voice should lift players, not direct them.
Substitutions: Rotate fairly and consistently. No child sits for more than one half. Subs should feel neutral — never a punishment.
Restart Management (U7-U8): Help players understand throw-ins and kick-ins. Keep restarts moving quickly — the goal is play time.
Injury Protocol: Stop play immediately. Stay calm. Assess. Contact the club immediately if anything is beyond a minor bump.
Wrap-Up: Bring your players together. One positive thing. Celebrate effort — never lead with the score.

Skill Tutorials for This Division

Inside Cut and Outside Cut
Video

The two most important direction changes in soccer. Master these and you can beat most defenders at this age.

Pull-Back Turn
Video

Step on top of ball, drag backward, turn and go. Use when a defender has closed you down. Essential escape move.

Push Pass Technique
Video

Locked ankle, non-kicking foot beside ball, strike through the middle. The foundation of every pass.

Receiving with Inside of Foot
Video

Cushion the ball on arrival. Soft touch, ball stays close. Practice against a wall or with a partner.

Shooting with Laces
Video

Plant foot beside ball, laces through the center, follow through toward target. Power comes from technique.

SafeSport Standards

These standards protect the players you work with and protect you.

Required Reading
US Soccer and CYA require all coaches to be familiar with SafeSport standards. These standards exist because the vast majority of harm to young athletes occurs in private, one-on-one situations. The standards below are designed to eliminate those opportunities entirely.
Two-Deep Leadership
Never be alone with a minor — ever. A minimum of two adults must be present at all practices, games, and events.
Open and Observable
All activities and conversations with players must occur in open, observable, interruptible settings. Closed vehicles and private spaces are never appropriate.
Electronic Communication
Do not send private one-to-one messages to minor players. All digital communication involving players should include a parent or guardian.
Appropriate Physical Contact
High-fives, fist bumps, and group celebrations are appropriate. Avoid extended physical contact. When in doubt, do not.
Transportation
Never transport a minor alone in a vehicle without another adult present.
Locker Rooms
Adults must never be alone with a minor in a locker room, bathroom, or changing area.
Reporting Obligations
If you observe, suspect, or are told about any form of abuse or boundary violation involving a minor, report it immediately. Contact your coordinator and relevant authorities. Do not investigate — report.
Your Own Protection
Following these standards protects the players you work with and protects you. A coach who follows them never has to worry about their own conduct being questioned.
If You Are Unsure
Ask yourself whether you would be comfortable if the player's parent could see exactly what you are doing and why. If yes: proceed. If no or maybe: do not do it. If you observe something concerning, report it.

Progressive Model (Unopposed → Opposed)▼
The Progressive Model (Unopposed → Opposed)

Used within the Practice phase of Play-Practice-Play. Every skill should be introduced simply and built toward game-realistic pressure in three steps.

1
UNOPPOSED
Technical Foundation. Player performs the skill without a defender. Focus purely on correct technique. No pressure, no decision-making yet.
Example: Dribble through cones · Pass against a wall · Shoot at empty goal
→
2
SEMI-OPPOSED
Decision-Making Introduced. A passive or shadow defender is added. The player reads a situation and makes a choice. Technique may break down — that is expected.
Example: 1v1 with a jog-only defender · Dribble with shadow who cannot tackle
→
3
FULLY OPPOSED
Game-Realistic Pressure. Live, competitive defender. Full pressure. Mistakes happen — use them as teaching moments. This is where learning is cemented.
Example: 1v1 live · Small-sided game · Match play

Common Mistake

Skipping Steps 1 and 2 because "it is just a rec session."

Even 5 minutes unopposed sets the tone, builds confidence, and gives your coaching points something to attach to.

Key Principle

Players need to feel success at each step before pressure is added.

Jumping to fully opposed work before technique is established creates frustration and sloppy habits. Build the foundation, then test it.

Recommended Session Structure

TimePhaseWhat to Do
10–15 min
Dynamic Warm-Up
Technical ball work with dynamic movement. Short rondo or passing pattern sets the tone.
20–25 min
Technical Phase
Focused skill practice. Unopposed first, then add progressive pressure.
15–20 min
Tactical Application
3v3 or 4v4 applying the technical theme with defined objectives.
25–30 min
Small-Sided Game (7v7)
Field: 60×40 yards. Standard rules. Brief coaching stoppages. Equal playing time always.
5 min
Cool Down and Review
Stretch and debrief. What went well? What is the next priority?

Key Reminders for This Age Group

Play-Practice-Play: game to identify needs → practice the skill → return to the game
Introduce the "why" behind tactical choices
Ask questions rather than giving answers
Rotate positions within every session — avoid early specialization

⚖ Working with Officials
The Foundational Rule
Your players are watching how you respond to every call. You are teaching them how to handle adversity, authority, and disappointment — not just soccer.
Before the Game
Introduce yourself and learn the official's name. This creates a positive dynamic for the entire game.
During the Game
Never argue a call. You may ask one polite question: "Can you help me understand that call?" Once, calmly, at a stoppage.
After a Difficult Call
Turn to your players: "Keep playing — we focus on what we control." How you respond teaches more than any call.
After the Game
A genuine thank-you to the official is good modeling for your players and more appreciated than you might expect.
Serious Procedural Errors
Address formally through your coordinator after the game. Never escalate on the field.
Player Reactions
Acknowledge the feeling, then redirect. "I hear you. Twenty minutes left — let's use them."
Parent Sideline Issues
Move to them immediately and quietly: "This stops now." Then document it and report to your coordinator.
Context
At recreational level, most officials are volunteers — often teenagers. They will make mistakes. Model grace. Your players are watching.
⚠ Conflict Resolution
The 3-Second Pause

Redirect to the activity quickly — dwelling extends the conflict
For U13+: short conversation in a visible, open area with another adult nearby

Script: "Hey [name], do you have a moment? I know you are passionate — that genuinely helps. When coaching from the sideline happens during the game, [child] receives conflicting instruction. Could we try cheering everyone during the game instead? I think it would really benefit [child]."
Discipline and Player Behavior▼
Natural consequences are always more effective than punitive consequences
Brief time-out (30–60 seconds) for young players — not extended benching
Never use physical exercise as punishment — this creates a lasting negative association with fitness
For U9+: short calm conversation in an open, observable area with another adult present
Document any serious incidents and share with your program coordinator
Consistent standards matter more than the severity of consequences
The goal is learning, growth, and continued engagement with the sport
Script: "I need to talk with you for a moment. What happened back there? ... I hear you. Here is what needs to be different: [specific behavior]. When we come back next week, we start fresh. Deal?"
Your Own Frustration as a Coach▼
You will have difficult days — that is human and nothing to be ashamed of
If emotions are rising, pause 10 seconds before responding — this is professionalism
Your emotional state affects every player on that field, whether you speak or not
No session outcome is worth damaging a player's relationship with the sport
After a difficult session, talk to a fellow coach or your coordinator — do not carry it alone
Self-compassion is required for sustained good coaching
Script: Remind yourself: "My primary job is to make sure they want to come back next week. Everything else is secondary."

SafeSport Standards

These standards protect the players you work with and protect you.

Required Reading
US Soccer and CYA require all coaches to be familiar with SafeSport standards. These standards exist because the vast majority of harm to young athletes occurs in private, one-on-one situations. The standards below are designed to eliminate those opportunities entirely.
Two-Deep Leadership
Never be alone with a minor — ever. A minimum of two adults must be present at all practices, games, and events.
Open and Observable
All activities and conversations with players must occur in open, observable, interruptible settings. Closed vehicles and private spaces are never appropriate.
Electronic Communication
Do not send private one-to-one messages to minor players. All digital communication involving players should include a parent or guardian.
Appropriate Physical Contact
High-fives, fist bumps, and group celebrations are appropriate. Avoid extended physical contact. When in doubt, do not.
Transportation
Never transport a minor alone in a vehicle without another adult present.
Locker Rooms
Adults must never be alone with a minor in a locker room, bathroom, or changing area.
Reporting Obligations
If you observe, suspect, or are told about any form of abuse or boundary violation involving a minor, report it immediately. Contact your coordinator and relevant authorities. Do not investigate — report.
Your Own Protection
Following these standards protects the players you work with and protects you. A coach who follows them never has to worry about their own conduct being questioned.
If You Are Unsure
Ask yourself whether you would be comfortable if the player's parent could see exactly what you are doing and why. If yes: proceed. If no or maybe: do not do it. If you observe something concerning, report it.
